Sunward ☀ — how it works

Finding New Zealand hikes that will actually be in the sun — by modelling the shadow of every hill, on every trail, at any hour.

The one idea that makes it work

In hilly country the sun can be shining while you're standing in cold shadow, because a ridge is in the way. Whether a spot is sunny depends on just two things:

1. The shape of the skyline around you — how high the land rises in every compass direction. This never changes.
2. Where the sun is — its height and compass bearing at a given date and time. This is simple astronomy.

A spot is in sunlight exactly when the sun sits higher than the skyline in the sun's direction. So we measure the skyline once for every 100 m of every track and store it. After that, "is this spot sunny at 10 am on 2 July?" is a single comparison — which is why Sunward can rank thousands of trails in the blink of an eye.

Sun vs. skyline, in one picture

For each trail point we send out 120 rays — one every 3° of compass — and march across the elevation model up to 16 km out, recording how high the land climbs. That gives a horizon profile: the angle of the skyline all the way around. The sun is "up" for you only when it clears that profile.

How the pieces are linked

A trail starts as a bare line on a map and is progressively enriched until it knows its own sunlight, shelter and weather.

  1. Collect & clean. National tracks are pulled in, duplicate copies of the same track are merged, and each line is sampled into points every ~100 m.
  2. Enrich. DOC's descriptions, difficulty grades and photos are matched onto each track by name + nearness, so a line becomes a described, graded, illustrated hike.
  3. Give it elevation & a skyline. Every 100 m point is looked up in the terrain model and its 120-direction horizon profile is ray-marched and stored.
  4. Tag the canopy. Points are tested against land-cover polygons to record how much of the walk is under bush, and whether it's native or plantation.
  5. Score on demand. When you search, the sun's position is computed for your date and time, checked against every point's stored skyline, blended with the live cloud forecast, and the trails are ranked.

The calculations, briefly

None of these are heavy at search time — the expensive terrain work was already done and stored. Query time is arithmetic.

☀ Is a point in sun?

in_sun(point, t)  =  sun_elevation(t) > horizon[point][ sun_azimuth(t) ]

The sun's height and bearing come from the NOAA solar-position formulas; the skyline height in that bearing is a stored lookup. One comparison per point.

◱ Terrain-sun score of a whole hike

terrain_sun  =  share of (every point × every 10 min of your window) that are in sun

Averaged over the trail and over your start-to-finish time window, so a hike that falls into shadow halfway is scored honestly.

⛅ Adding the weather

effective_sun  =  terrain_sun × ( 1 − 0.75 × cloud_fraction )

Cloud comes from the per-trail hourly forecast, weighted across your hike window. You can rank by terrain alone (great for planning far ahead) or by forecast-adjusted sun.

🥾 Walking time (Naismith's rule)

minutes  =  distance ÷ pace  +  ascent ÷ 10

A base pace set by the track's difficulty, plus one minute for every 10 m of climb — the climb read straight from the elevation model.

🌿 Canopy & difficulty

canopy = weighted share of points under forest  ·  difficulty = official grade, else derived from length + steepness

Forest classes are weighted (closed native forest counts more than low scrub) and the dominant type — native or exotic — is recorded for display.

The shadow map

Drag the time slider and the same ray-marching physics runs live in background threads (Web Workers), casting every hill's shadow across the terrain. It was cross-checked against the stored horizon engine and agreed on 96.9% of thousands of sampled points — so what you see on the map is the same truth used to rank the trails.
